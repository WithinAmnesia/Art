In Dungeons & Dragons, Human Dual-Classing (or Multiclassing) allows a human character to abandon their original class and start fresh in a new one, keeping their previous hit points while gaining new skills. These iconic rules trace back across several rulebooks and computer adaptations:1e & 2e (Advanced Dungeons & Dragons)
    Who wrote it: Gary Gygax authored 1e (1978), and Zeb Cook penned the 2e revision (1989), both for TSR.     The Rules:        Requirements: Your character must be Human. You need a 15+ in the prime attribute(s) of your first class and a 17+ in the prime attribute(s) of the new class.        Restrictions: You stop advancing in your first class. You are at level 0 in your new class (starting over at 0 XP).        The Catch: You lose the abilities of your first class entirely. If you use any of your old class skills in an adventure, you receive zero experience for that encounter.        The Payoff: Once your new class level exceeds your old class level, you permanently regain all of your original class abilities, and you can use both sets of skills (adhering to gear/armor restrictions). 
3e & 3.5e (D&D 3rd Edition)
    Who wrote it: Monte Cook, Jonathan Tweet, and Skip Williams (2000), published by Wizards of the Coast.     The Rules:        Multiclassing Replaces Dual-Classing: The rigid 1e/2e dual-classing was abolished. Humans and demi-humans alike now simply "Multiclass".        The Rules: When you level up, you can choose to take your next level in a brand-new class at Level 1, gaining all its features and Hit Dice.        The Catch: You must meet the primary attribute requirement (13+) for both classes. Furthermore, unless your classes are within 1 level of each other, you suffer a flat -20% penalty to all earned Experience Points (XP), though humans ignore this penalty for their highest-level class (their "favored class"). 
5e (5th Edition)
    Who wrote it: Jeremy Crawford and Mike Mearls (2014), published by Wizards of the Coast.     The Rules:        Optional Feature: Multiclassing is technically an optional rule that your Dungeon Master can allow.        The Rules: You can take a Level 1 dip into a new class whenever you level up, provided you have a 13+ in the core ability score of both your current class and the new class.        The Payoff: You immediately gain proficiencies associated with the new class (e.g., weapon/armor training), and you combine your spellcaster levels to determine your available spell slots. 
Baldur's Gate (BG)
    Who wrote it: BioWare and Beamdog, adapting the Advanced Dungeons & Dragons (2e) ruleset into a CRPG.     The Rules:        The Rules: The CRPG faithfully adapts the core 2e rules. You leave your old class abilities inactive, progress in your new class from Level 1, and regain your old skills once you surpass your old level.        The Restrictions: You must reach at least Level 2 in your initial class before dual-classing. Kits (such as Berserker or Wizard Slayer) can be dual-classed, but classes like Paladins, Bards, and Monks cannot be dual-classed at all. You are also bound by the game's overall Level limits.
